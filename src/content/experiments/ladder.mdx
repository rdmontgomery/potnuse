A small instrument for climbing the staff. Show a note — treble, bass, or grand — and name it. Miss it and it comes back within two or three rungs; get it right and it walks itself further out. Eight rungs of spacing between review and the next review, doubling each time you land the name clean.

[Climb the ladder →](/experiments/ladder)

## why a ladder

Spaced repetition is usually sold as a memory trick, but the shape of it is older than that. Vygotsky's zone of proximal development: you can only learn the next rung when someone — or something — holds the ladder steady one step above where you are right now. The interval schedule *is* the holding. It keeps pushing the next rung to the edge of comfort, never further.

So: not Anki with a music theme. A tool whose job is to find the rung you're on this minute and hand you the one above it.

## why not durations (yet)

Note-naming and duration-reading are different games. Naming is a lookup — what pitch is this glyph? Durations are a motor skill — can you feel the subdivision and count it while your eyes move? Mashing them into the same SRS loop would muddy what each review is measuring. A second piece for rhythm belongs nearby but not inside this one.

## where it might grow

- Key signatures as a mode (sharp and flat the same staff notes).
- Intervals: show two notes, name the interval.
- Chord recognition for the grand staff.
- Per-piece: load a score and let the SRS schedule drill sessions on the notes *that piece* actually contains. The ladder knows your weaknesses, so a piece becomes a personalized lesson plan.

All of which is why this stays its own thing rather than collapsing into a generic flashcard engine: the shape of the review — what counts as a card, how feedback is rendered, what mastery means — is specific to each of these games. Copy the Leitner bones when the next one shows up; don't abstract them early.
