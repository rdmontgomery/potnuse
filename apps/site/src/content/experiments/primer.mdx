Built for my daughter. She knows the alphabet — can name every letter cold — but the leap from naming to *reading* is its own ladder. So this is one: tap the picture that starts with this letter, or the picture that matches this word. Cards you miss return sooner; cards you land drift further out.

## four rungs

**letters** is letter-sound matching. A big *A a* sits on the card; three pictures sit underneath. The right answer is the one whose name begins with that sound. The card itself is silent until you ask — tap the speaker and a voice reads the exemplar slowly, then again at speed. Slow English speech stretches the leading phoneme on its own, so *hat* opens with an audible /h/ without us having to spell out a phoneme TTS engines can't say cleanly.

**words** is short-word recognition, picture-out. A three-letter word sits on the card; tap it and a voice reads it stretched then fluent — *caaat. cat.* Then pick the picture. CVC words mostly: cat, dog, pig, sun, bug, hat, bus. The kind of words a child can decode the first day she figures out blending.

**pictures** is the same game from the other end. An emoji sits on the card; the answers are written words. Tap the picture to hear what it is, then find the word that matches. Word-recognition rather than word-decoding — she's reading by shape, not by sounding out, which is the second way English actually gets read.

**spell** is the production mode. The card shows nothing but a speaker; tap it and a voice reads the word. Underneath are three (or more) letter tiles, scrambled. Drag them into the empty slots to spell what you heard. A *+ extras* toggle salts the pool with letters that don't belong, so she has to know not just the order but which letters are even in play. The first three modes ask her to *recognize*; this one asks her to *produce*.

## why these modes

Letter-naming and letter-sounding are the same skill in different clothes — she already names. Letter-sounding, word-decoding, word-recognition, and word-production are four different things: a lookup, a blend, a shape-match, a recall. The four modes are four cliffs.

Sight words come next, probably, when she's ready for *the*, *and*, *is* — words that don't decode cleanly and have to be memorized as shapes. They want their own mode rather than a fifth deck inside an existing one, because the feedback shape is different: no picture, no decoding, just the word and your voice naming it back.

## design notes

- **Pictures, not text, for answers.** A non-reader can't pick from `cat / dog / pig` written out — they'd be reading the answers. Emoji as low-cost iconography: no asset pipeline, recognizable, varied.
- **Audio is on demand, not automatic.** Auto-speech the moment a card appears trains the kid to wait for the voice instead of looking at the letter. The speak button is there for help; the card is the prompt.
- **Reinforcement after answer.** Right or wrong, the voice says what the card was. Wrong gets a longer pause before the next card so the correction lands.
- **Same SRS bones as [ladder](/experiments/ladder).** Leitner-style intervals, per-mode persistence to localStorage. Copy the bones; don't abstract them — the *shape* of a card here (prompt + three pictures) is different enough from naming a note that a shared engine would just be a thicker layer to pass everything through.
